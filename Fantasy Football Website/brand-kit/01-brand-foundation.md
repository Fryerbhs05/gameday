# Brand foundation

## What Conflicted is

Conflicted is a multi-league fantasy football tracker. The user runs two, three, or four fantasy leagues simultaneously and the same NFL player appears on multiple of their rosters — sometimes as their player, sometimes as their opponent's. On Sunday afternoon, when Justin Jefferson scores a touchdown, the user has no immediate way to know whether to cheer or curse. Conflicted answers that question in real time, league by league, player by player.

## Who it's for

The product is not for the casual fantasy player who runs one league with their college friends. It's for the *over-invested* fantasy player — typically a man, 30 to 55, who plays in three or more leagues a year, has been doing this for a decade-plus, and treats Sunday afternoon like an unpaid second job. He knows what RB1 means. He has opinions about ADP. He is mildly embarrassed about how much of his mental real estate this occupies, and he wants a tool that respects that he is too far in to play casually.

The brand should not feel like ESPN, DraftKings, or a casino. It should feel like a tool a serious hobbyist uses — closer to a fintech app or a well-designed productivity tool than a sports broadcast.

## The central design idea: the split

The single most important visual concept is the **vertical split**. The logo is bisected. The brand uses two colors, navy and coral, that are deliberately presented as opposing rather than harmonized. The product itself sorts players into camps — your guys, the enemy's guys, and the in-between. The split motif should appear throughout the UI wherever the product is showing dual loyalty, mixed status, or competing interests.

Concretely, the next agent should look for opportunities to:

- Bisect the player avatar or jersey number on a "split" status player (left half navy, right half coral)
- Use a thin vertical divider line in score-strip cells to signal "two leagues, two outcomes"
- Render percentage bars as two-tone (navy fill + coral fill meeting at the midpoint) rather than single-color progress bars
- Treat the favicon as a half-and-half C

The split is functional, not decorative. If a UI element doesn't represent any kind of duality, do not split it.

## Brand personality

In four adjectives: smart, dry, unbothered, slightly self-aware.

- **Smart** — the brand assumes the user already knows fantasy football. It does not over-explain. It uses real terminology.
- **Dry** — the tone is closer to a financial tool than a hype brand. It informs, it does not exclaim.
- **Unbothered** — the brand is not anxious to prove it's cool. No big animations, no gradients, no "let's go!" energy.
- **Slightly self-aware** — the product name is itself a little wry. It admits the absurdity of the situation it's solving. The brand can occasionally wink at the user, but never twice in a row.

In one sentence: *Conflicted is the brand a serious fantasy player would design if he were also a graphic designer.*

## What the brand is not

- Not a sportsbook (no neon, no glowing dollar signs, no "BET NOW")
- Not a fan brand (no team-specific iconography, no player photos used as decoration, no chest-thumping)
- Not a casual-gamer brand (no rounded mascots, no cartoon footballs with eyes, no sticker-style illustration)
- Not a productivity SaaS brand (no abstract gradient orbs, no "Welcome back, Matt 👋", no "Powered by AI")

The negative space here is wide on purpose. The brand sits in a deliberate gap between sports-network energy and software-tool restraint. Most adjacent products pick one side. Conflicted picks neither.

## The strategic role of the rebrand

The previous name "Game Day" was generic, claimed by a thousand other products, and said nothing about what made the tool different. "Conflicted" claims a feeling that no competitor is talking about. The visual identity needs to honor that — if the design looks like every other fantasy app, the strongest asset (the name) is wasted.

The next agent's job is not to make the site look "good" in the abstract. It's to make the site look like *Conflicted specifically*, in a way that no other fantasy product could legitimately copy without it looking borrowed.
